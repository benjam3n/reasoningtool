# PART 1: DEEP DECOMPOSITION

## Level 1 Decomposition

1. Define Your Game
2. Understand the Rules
3. Build Capability
4. Make Good Moves
5. Manage the Downside
6. Keep Score Honestly
7. Evolve the Game

## Level 2: Sub-decomposing each component

### 1. DEFINE YOUR GAME

| Sub-component | Description |
|---|---|
| 1.1 Values Clarification | What do you actually care about vs. what you've been told to care about? |
| 1.2 Identity Construction | Who are you choosing to be? (not: who were you born as) |
| 1.3 Domain Selection | Which arenas will you compete/participate in? (career, relationships, craft, community, health, adventure) |
| 1.4 Scope Calibration | How big is your game? (local/global, ambitious/sustainable, solo/collective) |
| 1.5 Constraint Acceptance | Which constraints are real and which are self-imposed? What are you willing to sacrifice? |
| 1.6 Timeframe Setting | What timescale are you playing on? (months, decades, legacy) |

**Key tension**: Most people inherit their game from family, culture, or peer group without ever making it explicit. The act of *choosing* your game is itself one of the highest-leverage things you can do — and one of the most psychologically difficult, because it means accepting that alternatives exist and you're responsible for your choice.

### 2. UNDERSTAND THE RULES

| Sub-component | Description |
|---|---|
| 2.1 Actual vs. Stated Rules | Every domain has official rules and real rules. The real rules are learned through observation, not instruction. |
| 2.2 Power Structures | Who controls outcomes? What are the real incentive structures? |
| 2.3 Probability & Variance | What's skill vs. luck? What's the variance structure of your domain? |
| 2.4 Feedback Mechanisms | How quickly and accurately does reality tell you whether your moves are working? |
| 2.5 Network Effects | How much does who-you-know matter? Where are the returns to social capital? |
| 2.6 Non-linearities | Where are the tipping points, thresholds, and phase transitions in your domain? |

**Key tension**: People either assume the rules are fair (naive) or assume they're completely rigged (cynical). The truth is structural: rules create differential advantages, and understanding the structure lets you position yourself where the rules work *for* you rather than against you.

### 3. BUILD CAPABILITY

| Sub-component | Description |
|---|---|
| 3.1 Health & Energy | The substrate everything else runs on. Chronic underinvestment is the norm. |
| 3.2 Skill Acquisition | Deliberate practice, learning curves, skill stacking (combining B+ skills into rare combinations) |
| 3.3 Relationships | Building trust, reciprocity, and genuine connection (not "networking") |
| 3.4 Financial Capital | Savings, income streams, reducing burn rate. Money is stored optionality. |
| 3.5 Knowledge & Mental Models | Understanding that transfers across domains. The latticework of models. |
| 3.6 Emotional Regulation | The ability to function well under stress, uncertainty, boredom, and temptation. |
| 3.7 Reputation & Credibility | What others believe about you. Built slowly, lost quickly. |

**Key tension**: Capability-building is boring and slow. It conflicts with the desire for immediate results. The people who build the deepest capability often look like they're "behind" for years before suddenly appearing to be ahead.

### 4. MAKE GOOD MOVES

| Sub-component | Description |
|---|---|
| 4.1 Decision Quality | Using good processes rather than relying on intuition alone |
| 4.2 Timing | When to act vs. when to wait. Patience is underrated; decisiveness is also underrated |
| 4.3 Execution Discipline | Doing what you decided to do, even when motivation fades |
| 4.4 Opportunity Recognition | Seeing asymmetric opportunities when they appear |
| 4.5 Resource Allocation | Where to spend your finite time, energy, money, and attention |
| 4.6 Communication | Persuasion, clarity, negotiation, conflict resolution |
| 4.7 Saying No | Most good moves are actually *refusals* — protecting time and focus from the merely good |

**Key tension**: The quality of individual moves matters far less than people think. A mediocre player with great game definition and honest feedback loops will beat a brilliant tactician playing the wrong game. But within the right game, move quality compounds.

### 5. MANAGE THE DOWNSIDE

| Sub-component | Description |
|---|---|
| 5.1 Financial Resilience | Emergency fund, insurance, low fixed costs, avoiding ruin |
| 5.2 Relationship Resilience | Not depending on any single person for all emotional needs |
| 5.3 Identity Resilience | Not attaching your worth to any single outcome or role |
| 5.4 Health Resilience | Margins in sleep, stress, physical capacity |
| 5.5 Optionality Preservation | Keeping doors open. Not making irreversible commitments casually |
| 5.6 Failure Processing | The ability to lose, grieve, learn, and re-engage without being destroyed |
| 5.7 Tail Risk Management | Avoiding catastrophic, unrecoverable losses (financial ruin, health destruction, relationship betrayal) |

**Key tension**: Upside-seeking and downside-managing use different psychological muscles. Optimism helps you take action; pessimism helps you survive. You need both, applied in the right contexts. Most people are temperamentally disposed toward one and underweight the other.

### 6. KEEP SCORE HONESTLY

| Sub-component | Description |
|---|---|
| 6.1 Choose Your Metrics | What counts as "winning" for *you*? Not the default social metrics. |
| 6.2 Avoid Goodhart's Law | When the measure becomes the target, it ceases to be a good measure |
| 6.3 Lead vs. Lag Indicators | Track inputs (behaviors) not just outputs (results). Results have variance; behaviors are controllable. |
| 6.4 Regular Review | Weekly, monthly, annual check-ins against your own criteria |
| 6.5 Honest Self-Assessment | Separating ego from evaluation. Can you see your own situation clearly? |
| 6.6 External Feedback | Seeking honest input from people who aren't invested in making you feel good |
| 6.7 Comparison Management | Comparing yourself to your past self rather than to curated versions of others |

**Key tension**: The scoring systems most available to you (social media, salary, status symbols) are the least likely to measure what actually matters to you. Building your own scoreboard requires both self-knowledge and the courage to deviate from consensus.

### 7. EVOLVE THE GAME

| Sub-component | Description |
|---|---|
| 7.1 Life Stage Transitions | What was right at 25 may be wrong at 45. Recognizing when your game needs to change. |
| 7.2 Sunk Cost Release | Letting go of paths you've invested in when they no longer serve you |
| 7.3 Identity Updates | Updating who you are as you learn and grow, without losing continuity |
| 7.4 Environmental Scanning | Noticing when the world changes in ways that affect your game |
| 7.5 Experimentation | Running small tests to discover what might be your next game |
| 7.6 Integration | Incorporating lessons from previous games into the next one |

**Key tension**: Evolution requires admitting your previous game was incomplete or wrong. This feels like failure. The reframe: a life that never evolves its game was either perfectly defined from the start (unlikely) or became rigid (common).

# PART 2: SYSTEMS ANALYSIS

## System Boundary

**System**: The life of one person ("the player") across all domains
**Time horizon**: Decades (a full adult life)
**Behavior to understand**: Why some people feel they're playing well and others don't, even at similar levels of external success

## Key Stocks

| Stock | Inflows | Outflows | Typical Fill Time |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Health** | Exercise, sleep, nutrition, medical care | Aging, stress, illness, neglect | Months to build, days to damage |
| **Skill** | Deliberate practice, experience | Atrophy, irrelevance | Years to build, years to lose |
| **Relationships** | Time invested, trust, vulnerability | Neglect, betrayal, life changes | Years to build, moments to lose |
| **Financial Capital** | Income, returns | Spending, losses, inflation | Decades to build, moments to lose |
| **Reputation** | Consistent delivery, integrity | Mistakes, inconsistency | Years to build, moments to lose |
| **Self-Knowledge** | Reflection, feedback, experience | Self-deception, avoidance | Lifelong accumulation |
| **Optionality** | Skills, savings, relationships, health | Commitments, debt, specialization | Variable |
| **Meaning** | Alignment between action and values | Drift, misalignment, numbness | Fluctuates |

**Critical observation**: Almost all key life stocks share an asymmetry — they are **slow to build and fast to lose**. This has profound implications for strategy: protection of existing stocks is often more valuable than aggressive pursuit of new ones.

## Feedback Loops

### Reinforcing Loops (R)

**R1: Capability Compound**
```
Skill -> (+) Better outcomes -> (+) More opportunities -> (+) More practice -> (+) Skill
```
*This is "success breeds success." The rich get richer in capability.*

**R2: Relationship Compound**
```
Good relationships -> (+) Opportunities -> (+) Resources -> (+) Ability to help others -> (+) Good relationships
```
*Social capital compounds. People who are generous and capable attract more of both.*

**R3: Confidence-Action Loop**
```
Confidence -> (+) Action-taking -> (+) Results -> (+) Confidence
```
*Works in both directions: confidence paralysis is the vicious version.*

**R4: Meaning-Energy Loop**
```
Meaning -> (+) Energy -> (+) Effort -> (+) Results -> (+) Meaning
```
*When you're playing a game that matters to you, effort doesn't feel like effort.*

**R5: Drift Loop (Vicious)**
```
Misalignment -> (+) Dissatisfaction -> (+) Avoidance behaviors -> (+) Less reflection -> (+) More misalignment
```
*Playing the wrong game creates psychological distance that prevents you from noticing you're playing the wrong game.*

### Balancing Loops (B)

**B1: Success Saturation**
```
Success in one area -> (+) Time/energy invested -> (-) Time for other areas -> (-) Balance -> (-) Sustainable success
```
*Over-optimizing on career kills relationships. Over-optimizing on relationships can kill growth. The system pushes back.*

**B2: Hedonic Adaptation**
```
Achievement -> (+) Satisfaction -> (+) New baseline -> (-) Marginal satisfaction from same level -> (-) Motivation
```
*The treadmill. Each win raises the bar for what counts as winning.*

**B3: Social Comparison Thermostat**
```
Achievement -> (+) Move to higher-status peer group -> (+) New comparison targets -> (-) Relative standing -> (-) Satisfaction
```
*No matter how much you achieve, your reference group adjusts.*

**B4: Complexity Tax**
```
More commitments -> (+) Coordination cost -> (-) Quality of each commitment -> (-) Satisfaction
```
*Life gets worse when you say yes to too many things, even good things.*

## System Archetypes Present

### Archetype 1: LIMITS TO GROWTH
- **R**: Capability compound drives career/skill growth
- **B**: Time, energy, and attention are finite. Health degrades under sustained pressure.
- **Leverage**: Identify the binding constraint *before* it bites. Invest in the constraint (usually health, relationships, or rest) while the growth loop is still working.

### Archetype 2: SHIFTING THE BURDEN
- **Symptom**: Dissatisfaction, lack of meaning
- **Quick fix**: Status consumption, comparison, distraction (social media, shopping, substances)
- **Fundamental solution**: Redefine the game, align action with values
- **Side effect**: Quick fixes reduce the urgency to do the fundamental work, and can create dependency
- **Leverage**: Invest in the fundamental solution (game redefinition). Recognize quick fixes as symptoms, not solutions.

### Archetype 3: SUCCESS TO THE SUCCESSFUL
- **Two competing "projects"**: External achievement vs. Inner development
- **Dynamic**: External achievement gets all the resources because it has legible rewards (money, status, praise). Inner development starves because its rewards are illegible.
- **Leverage**: Create separate "resource pools" — dedicated time for inner work that can't be cannibalized by external demands.

### Archetype 4: GROWTH AND UNDERINVESTMENT
- **Growth**: Ambition and capability drive bigger roles, more responsibility
- **Underinvestment**: In recovery, relationships, health, self-knowledge
- **Performance degradation**: Burnout, relationship failure, health crisis
- **Self-fulfilling**: "I can't afford to rest" becomes true because you've never invested in the capacity to sustain effort
- **Leverage**: Invest ahead of demand. Build recovery and relationship capacity *before* you need it.

## Leverage Points (ranked)

| Rank | Leverage Point | Level | Intervention |
|---|---|---|---|
| **1** | Paradigm: "What game am I playing?" | Paradigm/Mindset | Explicit game definition and periodic redefinition |
| **2** | Goals: What you're optimizing for | System Goals | Choosing your own metrics rather than inheriting social defaults |
| **3** | Rules: Personal rules and commitments | System Rules | Designing your own constraints (e.g., "I don't work weekends", "I exercise before I check email") |
| **4** | Information flows: Honest feedback | Information | Building feedback loops that tell you the truth, not what you want to hear |
| **5** | Reinforcing loops: Capability compound | R-Loop Strength | Investing in skills that compound across domains |
| **6** | Balancing loops: Recovery systems | B-Loop Strength | Sleep, exercise, relationship maintenance as non-negotiable |
| **7** | Delays: Reducing lag between action and feedback | Delays | Weekly reviews, journaling, mentorship (faster signal) |
| **8** | Buffer: Financial and emotional margin | Buffer Size | Emergency fund, low fixed costs, emotional support network |

# PART 3: ASSUMPTION EXTRACTION

## Core Claims Embedded in "Playing a Good Game in Life"

**Claim 1**: Life can be usefully modeled as a game
**Claim 2**: "Good" play is definable and recognizable
**Claim 3**: Individual agency is the primary determinant of outcomes
**Claim 4**: There are better and worse strategies
**Claim 5**: Self-knowledge enables better play
**Claim 6**: The game can be won (or at least played well)

```
CLAIM 6: "The game can be 'won'"
|-- A6.1 There is a meaningful sense of success in life (EXISTENCE, Shallow)
|-- A6.2 Success is achievable, not just asymptotic (STABILITY, Deep)
|-- A6.3 Winning doesn't require others to lose (VALUE, Deep)
```

## Priority Assumptions (most critical to verify)

**1. A1.4**: "The game metaphor doesn't distort important aspects of life"
- Risk: HIGH. Games imply competition, optimization, winners/losers. Life includes grief, love, mystery, surrender — things that resist gamification. Over-indexing on the game metaphor may cause you to treat sacred things instrumentally.
- Test: Ask whether the metaphor improves your relationship with difficulty or makes it more adversarial.

**2. A3.3**: "Systemic factors are secondary to strategy"
- Risk: HIGH. This is survivorship bias territory. For someone born into extreme poverty, conflict, or severe disability, individual strategy has far less explanatory power. The "game" metaphor flatters those with already-good starting positions.
- Test: Would this framework feel true to someone with radically different starting conditions?

**3. A2.4**: "Good strategies reliably produce better outcomes"
- Risk: MEDIUM-HIGH. In high-variance domains (entrepreneurship, creative work, markets), strategy explains a minority of outcome variance. The relationship between strategy and result is real but noisy.
- Test: Look at the variance in outcomes among people with similar strategies.

**4. A4.2**: "What works is knowable in advance"
- Risk: MEDIUM. Hindsight bias makes every outcome look like it was predictable. Many "strategies" are just post-hoc narratives imposed on lucky breaks.
- Test: Can you predict outcomes for others using your framework, or only explain your own past?

# PART 4: ASSUMPTION INVERSION

## Inversion 1: "What if life is NOT usefully modeled as a game?"

**Inverted statement**: Life is better understood as a story, a mystery, an unfolding, or a practice — not a game.

**If true**:
- Optimization is the wrong frame. Presence and acceptance matter more than strategy.
- "Moves" become "responses." You're not trying to win; you're trying to be faithful to something.
- Failure isn't a bad move — it's part of the narrative.
- The Stoics, contemplatives, and artists have it more right than the strategists.

**Plausibility**: 50%. Many wisdom traditions explicitly reject the game frame.

**Blind spot revealed**: The game metaphor may systematically undervalue experiences that resist optimization — grief, awe, love, rest, play-for-its-own-sake. These aren't "moves." They're the texture of being alive.

**Implication**: Hold the game metaphor lightly. Use it where it's useful (capability-building, decision-making, resource allocation) and set it down for domains where it doesn't (intimacy, grief, spiritual life, play).

## Inversion 2: "What if individual agency is NOT primary?"

**Inverted statement**: Outcomes are primarily determined by systems, luck, timing, and starting conditions. Individual strategy is a minor factor.

**If true**:
- The right response is positioning, not optimization. Put yourself where the wind blows favorably.
- Community, collective action, and system change matter more than personal development.
- Self-help is a distraction from structural reform.
- Humility about your own contribution to your success is essential.

**Plausibility**: 40-60% depending on domain and starting conditions.

**Blind spot revealed**: The "playing a good game" frame can be a vehicle for meritocratic mythology — the belief that people get what they deserve. It can generate blame for the disadvantaged and unearned pride for the advantaged.

**Implication**: Combine personal strategy with structural awareness. Acknowledge luck. Use advantage to change systems, not just to win within them.

## Inversion 3: "What if you CAN'T know what 'good' means for you?"

**Inverted statement**: Self-knowledge is limited and unreliable. You can't know what you truly want until after you've tried it. Preferences are constructed, not discovered.

**If true**:
- Planning is overrated; experimentation is underrated.
- Commitment to a fixed game definition is premature — stay fluid longer.
- "Defining your game" becomes an ongoing experiment, not a one-time exercise.
- The process of living IS the discovery. You can't plan your way to self-knowledge.

**Plausibility**: 60%. Psychological research strongly supports this — people are bad at affective forecasting. What you think will make you happy often doesn't.

**Blind spot revealed**: The decomposition assumes self-knowledge precedes action. In reality, action often precedes self-knowledge. You learn what you value by doing things, not by introspecting.

**Implication**: Bias toward action and experimentation over planning and reflection. Use reflection to *process* experience, not to *replace* it.

## Inversion 4: "What if the game cannot be 'won'?"

**Inverted statement**: Life is an infinite game. The point is to keep playing, not to win. "Winning" is a category error.

**If true** (drawing from James Carse's *Finite and Infinite Games*):
- The goal shifts from achieving outcomes to sustaining play.
- Surprise, growth, and continuation matter more than accumulation.
- The best "move" is the one that opens the most interesting future moves, not the one that captures the most value.
- "Losing" is only quitting the game or making it impossible to continue.

**Plausibility**: 70%. This reframe is more honest than the finite game version for most of what life actually is.

**Blind spot revealed**: The original decomposition is biased toward finite game thinking — defining, optimizing, scoring, winning. Life has finite game elements (getting a job, passing an exam) nested within an infinite game structure (becoming who you're becoming). Confusing the two is a major failure mode.

**Implication**: Reframe from "playing a good game" to "sustaining good play." The emphasis shifts from outcomes to process, from achievement to growth, from winning to continuation.

# PART 5: CROSS-DOMAIN ANALOGY

## Abstracted Problem
"An agent with limited resources operating in a complex, partially observable environment with long time horizons, trying to produce outcomes they value while avoiding catastrophic failure."

## Top Analogies

### Analogy 1: POKER (Game Theory / Probabilistic Decision-Making)

| Life | Poker |
|---|---|
| Talent, health, starting conditions | Cards dealt |
| Strategy, decisions | Betting strategy |
| Career, relationship, creative domains | Tournaments entered |
| Luck, randomness | Card distribution |
| Reputation, relationships | Table image |
| Life stages | Blind levels increasing |

**How great poker players play:**
1. **Position selection** — Choose your table before you play your hand. The best players spend as much time choosing where to play as how to play.
2. **Bankroll management** — Never risk ruin. Survive long enough for skill to matter.
3. **Expected value, not outcomes** — Judge decisions by process, not results. A good bet that loses is still a good bet.
4. **Tilt management** — Emotional regulation after bad beats is a core skill.
5. **Range thinking** — Don't fixate on one outcome. Think in probability distributions.
6. **Table selection > hand selection > hand playing** — Strategy hierarchy mirrors life: which game you're in > which opportunities you pursue > how you execute.

**Transferable insight**: **Table selection is the highest-leverage decision.** Most people spend 90% of their effort on playing their hand better and 10% on choosing the right table. Invert that ratio.

### Analogy 2: ECOLOGY (Complex Adaptive Systems)

| Life | Ecology |
|---|---|
| Person | Organism |
| Skills, health, knowledge | Fitness / adaptive traits |
| Social environment | Ecosystem |
| Career changes, life transitions | Niche transitions |
| Culture, institutions | Environmental conditions |
| Aging, changing world | Shifting climate |

**How thriving organisms "play":**
1. **Niche construction** — Don't just adapt to the environment; modify it. Build the conditions that favor your strengths.
2. **Symbiosis** — The most successful organisms have deep mutualistic relationships. Pure competition is less common than cooperation.
3. **Antifragility through redundancy** — Biological systems maintain spare capacity. Efficiency is sacrificed for resilience.
4. **Seasonal strategy** — Different strategies for different conditions. Hibernation, migration, and flowering are all valid.
5. **Co-evolution** — You and your environment shape each other. You can't analyze yourself separately from your context.

**Transferable insight**: **Niche construction > niche adaptation.** Don't just find the right game — shape it. Create conditions where your particular combination of strengths is maximally valuable. Most people adapt to environments; the best players construct environments.

### Analogy 3: JAZZ IMPROVISATION (Creative Performance)

| Life | Jazz |
|---|---|
| Daily actions | Musical phrases |
| Values, identity | Musical voice |
| Skills, knowledge | Technique, theory |
| Other people | Band members |
| Life circumstances | Harmonic context |
| Long-term trajectory | Musical career/legacy |

**How great jazz musicians play:**
1. **Deep structure + freedom** — Master the fundamentals so thoroughly that improvisation becomes natural. You need structure to be free within it.
2. **Listening > playing** — The best improvisers spend more attention listening to what's happening around them than executing their own ideas.
3. **Respond to what IS, not what you planned** — When the music goes somewhere unexpected, follow it. Don't force your predetermined solo.
4. **Taste > technique** — What you leave out matters as much as what you play. Restraint and space are skills.
5. **Play with people better than you** — The fastest way to improve is to be the weakest person in a strong group.

**Transferable insight**: **Responsiveness matters more than planning.** Life rewards people who can respond to what's actually happening over people who execute brilliant plans for circumstances that no longer exist. Build responsiveness, not just strategy.

## Cross-Pollination

```
From Poker:   Table selection is the highest-leverage decision
From Ecology: Don't just adapt — construct your niche
From Jazz:    Responsiveness > predetermined strategy

COMBINED INSIGHT:
The best players in life practice "responsive niche construction" —
they choose their game carefully (poker), shape the environment to
favor their strengths (ecology), and stay responsive to what's
actually happening rather than rigidly executing plans (jazz).

This is fundamentally different from the dominant self-help model
which is: set goals -> make plans -> execute with discipline.

The better model is: choose your arena -> shape it -> respond to it ->
evolve with it.
```
