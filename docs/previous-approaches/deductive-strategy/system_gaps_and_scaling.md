An honest assessment of what the deductive strategy system can and can't do, and what's needed to make it complete and trustworthy.

## Current Gaps

What the system CANNOT do:

### Gap 1: Generate Correct Domain-Specific Strategies

The system can evaluate whether a strategy is well-reasoned.
The system CANNOT tell you what the correct strategy is for a specific domain.

**Example:**
- "What should I do to improve my breathing?" → System can't answer
- "Is this breathing strategy well-reasoned?" → System can help evaluate

**What's needed:**
- Domain expertise (physiology, medicine)
- Empirical data (what works for whom)
- Personal data (your specific situation)
- Causal models (what causes breathing problems, what interventions work)

### Gap 2: Connect Abstract Goals to Concrete Reality

The system can decompose goals into subgoals with nuance.
The system CANNOT automatically connect high-level goals to specific actions in your context.

**Example:**
- "Address wealth inequality" → decomposes to subgoals
- But: which specific intervention, in your location, with your resources, for which population?

**What's needed:**
- Context-specific information (local conditions, available resources)
- Opportunity mapping (what's actually possible here and now)
- Constraint identification (what's blocking action)
- Personal fit assessment (what you specifically can do)

### Gap 3: Validate Its Own Outputs

The system can produce recommendations.
The system CANNOT verify those recommendations are correct without external feedback.

**Example:**
- System recommends strategy X
- How do you know X is better than Y?
- How do you know X will actually work?

**What's needed:**
- Testing mechanisms (try and see)
- Comparison benchmarks (against alternatives)
- Outcome tracking (did it work?)
- Feedback integration (update based on results)

### Gap 4: Handle Your Specific Situation

The system is general-purpose.
The system CANNOT automatically account for your unique circumstances.

**Example:**
- Generic advice: "Exercise regularly"
- Your situation: specific health conditions, constraints, preferences, past attempts, what's worked/failed

**What's needed:**
- Personal history integration
- Individual constraint mapping
- Past attempt analysis (what failed and why)
- Personalized recommendation adjustment

### Gap 5: Produce Causal Models

The system knows causation matters.
The system CANNOT generate causal models from first principles.

**Example:**
- "Why am I having trouble with conversations?"
- Could be: attention regulation, anxiety, lack of practice, social context, neurological factors, skill gaps, etc.
- System can list these but can't determine which is true for you

**What's needed:**
- Diagnostic frameworks
- Empirical investigation
- Testing and iteration
- Domain knowledge about what causes what

### Gap 6: Integrate With GOSM Infrastructure

The system is a document describing thinking.
The system IS NOT integrated into GOSM's actual operation.

**What's needed:**
- Integration with GOSM's 22-step process
- Hooks into context.json and tracking
- Automated question surfacing
- Review automation

**Abstract goal:** "Improve physical health"

**What the system can do:**
- Decompose into dimensions (breathing, weight, cardiovascular, etc.)
- Prioritize by criteria (ease, impact, etc.)
- Structure investigation process
- Provide review framework

**What the system cannot do without additional work:**
- Tell you what specifically is wrong
- Recommend specific interventions
- Predict what will work for you

**What's needed:**
- Medical information (diagnosis, options)
- Personal data (your body, your history)
- Experimentation (try things, observe results)
- Domain expertise (what the science actually says)

**The system's role:** Structure the process of acquiring and applying that information.

### Example: Wealth Inequality

**Abstract goal:** "Address wealth inequality"

**What the system can do:**
- Decompose with nuance (material, opportunity, power, dignity)
- Identify scope and constraints
- Apply prioritization
- Surface tradeoffs

**What the system cannot do without additional work:**
- Tell you which intervention is most effective
- Map the specific political/economic landscape
- Identify your specific leverage points
- Predict effects of interventions

**What's needed:**
- Economics knowledge (what causes inequality, what reduces it)
- Political knowledge (what's feasible)
- Local knowledge (your specific context)
- Position analysis (what you can actually do)

**The system's role:** Structure the process of understanding and acting.

### Example: "Returning To Nature"

**Abstract goal:** "Return to nature in some respects"

**What the system can do:**
- Ask what "return to nature" actually means
- Decompose dimensions (physical environment, lifestyle, values, connection)
- Identify conflicts (modern life dependencies vs nature goals)
- Prioritize actionable elements

**What the system cannot do without additional work:**
- Define what "nature" means to you specifically
- Map practical options in your location
- Resolve the tension between modern necessities and nature goals
- Tell you how much nature contact produces what benefits

**What's needed:**
- Value clarification (what do you actually want from "nature")
- Practical options (what's possible where you are)
- Science on nature benefits (what actually helps)
- Personal experimentation (what works for you)

**The system's role:** Structure the clarification and exploration.

## The Trust Threshold

### What Needs To Happen For You To Trust The System

Based on the conversation history, you need:

1. **One real win** - Evidence that following this produces better outcomes than not

2. **Self-evident correctness** - The strategy feels logically necessary, not just "could work"

3. **No guessing** - Clear reasoning chain from problem to solution

4. **Personal fit** - Accounts for your specific situation, not generic advice

5. **Robust under scrutiny** - Survives hard questioning, doesn't fall apart

### How To Get There

**Phase 1: Apply to one real goal**
- Choose a goal (health improvement, given its leverage)
- Apply full process
- Document everything
- Track outcomes

**Phase 2: Validate or invalidate**
- Did the system produce different recommendations than intuition?
- Were the recommendations better?
- What evidence accumulated?
- What failed and why?

**Phase 3: Iterate**
- Update system based on learning
- Try again
- Accumulate evidence

**Phase 4: Extend**
- If validated on one goal, try another
- If invalidated, analyze why and fix
- Build track record

### The Minimum Viable Test

Pick one concrete goal with:
- Measurable outcomes
- Relatively fast feedback
- Something you were going to try anyway

Apply the system. Document predictions. Track results. Compare to baseline.

If the system produces better outcomes than winging it, that's evidence of value.
If it doesn't, analyze why and improve or abandon.

## Honest Assessment

### What This System Is

A framework for thinking about how to think about goals and strategies.

It's like having a map of what questions to ask and in what order.

It's NOT a map of the territory (the specific domain knowledge).
It's NOT an oracle that tells you what to do.
It's NOT proven to produce better outcomes.

### What Would Make It Valuable

If using the framework produces better outcomes than not using it.

This is an empirical question. It requires testing.

### What Would Make It Worthless

If using the framework produces the same or worse outcomes than intuition.

Or if the overhead of using it exceeds the benefit.

Or if it can't be made practical.

### The Next Step

Apply to one real goal. Find out.
